How The Apps Changed

OpenAssistant

OpenAssistant was the first version. I built it because I wanted a better way to work through documents on iPhone. The path in was the Assistants API, copied docs, Xcode errors, and a lot of iteration. It also got rejected around 30 times before it stayed on the App Store.

OpenCone

OpenCone was the next step once bigger document sets started stressing the earlier workflow. I wanted more retrieval control, found Pinecone, and built around indexes and namespaces. Getting it onto the App Store was mostly me wanting to see if I could do it again.

OpenResponses

OpenResponses was the rebuild after OpenAI moved to Responses. I did not want the first app stuck on older endpoints, so I rebuilt the core flow and got it through review on the first submission.

OpenIntelligence

OpenIntelligence came after WWDC25 exposed Foundation Models. I wanted to try the same kind of document workflow on Apple's local model path. The big constraint was the 4,096-token session budget, which pushed me into a multi-session reasoning loop.

OpenResponses

OpenResponses was the rebuild once it became obvious OpenAssistant was going to age out on older plumbing.

  • Why it exists: I did not want the first app to become nonfunctional or stuck behind outdated endpoints.
  • How I built it: I had OpenAssistant open in one window, OpenResponses in another, and rebuilt the core flow piece by piece on the Responses stack.
  • What changed: This time I was also aiming to get through App Review on the first pass instead of another rejection marathon.
  • Why it matters: It kept the app current and passed review on the first submission.

OpenIntelligence

OpenIntelligence came from wanting an offline version of the same document workflow. Once WWDC25 made Apple's Foundation Models path real for developers, I wanted to see what that idea looked like on-device.

  • Why it exists: I wanted the same grounded document workflow, but offline and built around Apple's on-device model path.
  • How it works: Local ingestion, evidence retrieval, source-backed answers, and explicit abstention when the material does not actually support the claim.
  • What got hard: Apple's public on-device sessions are capped at 4,096 tokens, and that budget has to cover instructions, retrieved context, tool and schema overhead, and the answer itself. That is what pushed me into a recursive multi-session reasoning loop.
  • Why it matters: It is the same document problem again, just in an offline, on-device form.

Docs GitHub App Store

OpenCone

OpenCone came from pushing the same document problem further once larger queries and bigger document sets started making the earlier flow feel unstable.

  • Why it exists: I wanted to work with more material than the Playground and Assistants flow handled comfortably.
  • How I built it: Almost the same way as the first app, except this time Pinecone docs were in the pile too and I was learning indexes, namespaces, and embeddings on the fly.
  • What changed: More room, more retrieval control, and less chance of the whole thing bugging out.
  • Why it matters: Getting it onto the App Store was personal proof that I could keep pushing this further.

OpenAssistant

OpenAssistant started as a personal hurdle. LLMs made dense docs way easier to work through, but I wanted more control over the workflow and how much material I could throw at it.

  • Why it exists: The original problem was simple: I wanted an iPhone version that fit how I actually used documentation, not just whatever the official app happened to allow.
  • How I built it: I fed API docs into text files, used the Playground like crazy, pasted red Xcode errors back into the loop, and kept doing that until the app finally ran.
  • What got in the way: Around 30 App Review rejections forced me to learn review, polish, and edge-case cleanup while I was still learning iOS itself.
  • Why it matters: It is still the foundation for everything after it.
