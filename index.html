My Story

My plan changed, then changed, then changed again.

I grew up thinking I’d be a cop. Turns out I hated the idea of spending my life handing out tickets. Swapped majors, got an exercise science degree, spent a few years coaching people who mostly wanted quick fixes. The truth: you can hand people every tool, but if they don’t want it, you’re just spinning your wheels. That drove me crazy.

I bailed on that, took a utility locator job. It paid the bills, but it also burned me out—10-hour days outside, no sense of progress, just the kind of tired that makes new ideas impossible. My brother and I talked about learning to code, but the energy wasn’t there. I kept thinking: there’s got to be something I can actually build and own.

What changed? World of Warcraft: Classic. Not a career move—a lifeline. I met my wife in that game. Moved across the country for her. Still the best decision I’ve ever made.

California. Took another locating gig—ground-penetrating radar. Sounded cool, wasn’t. Culture sucked, bailed quick.

Found Stryker by chance. The interview was wild—guy looked me in the eye and basically said: “You’re 30, no real career, what are you doing?” No idea. But I knew I wanted a shot at something real. They gave me the keys to a totally new role, zero training, no safety net, just me and a pile of surgical tech nobody else could figure out.

Three years in, I became the person surgeons call when the room’s on fire. Built Python tools to automate all the annoying stuff. Rolled out AI before anyone around me knew what the hell it was. I turned chaos into routines. I made my own leverage, because nobody was coming to save me.

Now? I build things because it keeps me sane. AI apps, automation, whatever solves an actual problem. I like feeling useful. I want to work with people who are hungry and honest, who want to make real things that matter—not just show off for LinkedIn.

That’s the signal. I want to work with people who get it.

Where I am now: Grateful for what I have and thoroughly enjoying my current role at Stryker. Recently, I've had a surge in energy that I have been pouring into my work and personal projects. As fun and fulfilling as it's been, it's gotten to the point where I've deliberately re-evaluated my goals and aspirations to match this newfound momentum.

I've come to realize that I want to be at the forefront of this technological revolution - not only because it's fascinating, but because I feel like I can contribute to its potential to transform the world in positive ways (while also considering its ethical implications).
